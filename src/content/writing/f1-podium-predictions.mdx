Formula 1 generates an extraordinary volume of data per race weekend — each car produces several gigabytes of telemetry across practice, qualifying, and the race itself. For ISYE 7406 (Data Mining & Statistical Learning) at Georgia Tech, I treated this as a structured prediction problem: given telemetry features sampled at the lap level, can we reliably predict lap time and podium probability?

## The Data

The dataset was constructed from multi-season lap-level records, with features including speed trace summaries, throttle and brake application fractions, DRS activation laps, tyre compound and age, fuel load estimate, and track sector times. After cleaning, the working set contained approximately 12,000 lap records across 22 circuits.

## Dimensionality Reduction with PCA

Raw telemetry produces highly correlated features — throttle and speed, for instance, are tightly coupled. I applied PCA to reduce the feature space while retaining 95% of explained variance. This compressed ~40 engineered features down to 14 principal components without meaningful information loss, and improved downstream model stability.

## Modelling

I trained and compared four models on the PCA-reduced features: Lasso Regression, Ridge Regression, Random Forest Regressor, and Gradient Boosting Regressor. All models were evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation using RMSE as the primary metric.

Random Forest delivered the best out-of-sample performance. Feature importance analysis (on the original feature space, via permutation importance) identified tyre age, fuel load, and cornering speed as the three strongest predictors — results consistent with what teams and commentators describe anecdotally.

## Podium Classification

Using the same feature pipeline, I reframed the problem as binary classification: will this driver finish on the podium? The class imbalance (~15% positive rate) required care — I used class-weight rebalancing rather than oversampling, and evaluated models on AUC-ROC rather than raw accuracy.
