Predicting football match outcomes is a canonical multi-class classification problem, and a notoriously difficult one. Draws in particular are under-predicted by almost every published model — the signal is weak, and the base rate is roughly 25% in the Premier League. For ISYE 6740 (Computational Data Analysis) at Georgia Tech, I treated this as a rigorous applied classification exercise.

## Feature Engineering and EDA

The dataset spanned multiple Premier League seasons and included match-level statistics: possession percentages, shots and shots on target, expected goals (xG and xGA), corners, fouls, yellow and red cards, and form metrics (points per game over the prior five matches).

Exploratory analysis revealed strong collinearity between possession and pass accuracy, and between xG and shots on target. Correlated features were pruned before modelling to avoid variance inflation. The final feature set used 18 variables.

## Model Comparison

Three classifiers were evaluated:

- **Logistic Regression** (multinomial, L2 regularization) — the interpretable baseline
- **Random Forest** — tuned via grid search on `n_estimators` and `max_depth`
- **Gradient Boosting** — tuned on `learning_rate` and `n_estimators`

All models were evaluated on a stratified 20% holdout with 10-fold cross-validation on the training set. Class imbalance was handled through `class_weight='balanced'` rather than synthetic oversampling.

## Results

Gradient Boosting achieved the highest overall accuracy (62.4%) and the best F1 score on the win class. All models struggled with draws, which is consistent with the published literature — the draw class is inherently low-signal and outcome-dependent on match state in the final 20 minutes, which static pre-match features cannot capture.

Defensive metrics — specifically xGA (expected goals against) and goals conceded in the previous five matches — were the strongest predictors for away teams, while home form and xG differential drove home-team predictions.
